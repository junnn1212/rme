A.1 Elementary Algebra

Mastery of Elementary Algebra (a.k.a. “College Algebra”) is a prerequisite for calculus, which is a prerequisite for Epi 202 and Epi 203, which are prerequisites for this course (Epi 204). Nevertheless, each year, some Epi 204 students are still uncomfortable with algebraic manipulations of mathematical formulas. Therefore, I include this section as a quick reference.

A.1.1 Equalities

Theorem A.1 (Equalities are transitive) If \(a=b\) and \(b=c\), then \(a=c\)

Theorem A.2 (Substituting equivalent expressions) If \(a = b\), then for any function \(f(x)\), \(f(a) = f(b)\)

A.1.2 Inequalities

Theorem A.3 If \(a<b\), then \(a+c < b+c\)

Theorem A.4 (negating both sides of an inequality) If \(a < b\), then: \(-a > -b\)

Theorem A.5 If \(a < b\) and \(c \geq 0\), then \(ca < cb\).

Theorem A.6 \[-a = (-1)*a\]

A.1.3 Sums

Theorem A.7 (adding zero changes nothing) \[a+0=a\]

Theorem A.8 (Sums are symmetric) \[a+b = b+a\]

Theorem A.9 (Sums are associative)  

When summing three or more terms, the order in which you sum them does not matter:

\[(a + b) + c = a + (b + c)\]

A.1.4 Products

Theorem A.10 (Multiplying by 1 changes nothing) \[a \times 1 = a\]

Theorem A.11 (Products are symmetric) \[a \times b = b \times a\]

Theorem A.12 (Products are associative) \[(a \times b) \times c = a \times (b \times c)\]

A.1.5 Sums and products together

Theorem A.13 (Multiplication is distributive) \[a(b+c) = ab + ac\]

A.1.6 Quotients

Definition A.1 (Quotients, fractions, rates)  

A quotient, fraction, or rate is a division of one quantity by another:

\[\frac{a}{b}\]

In epidemiology, rates typically have a quantity involving time or population in the denominator.

c.f. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rate_(mathematics)

Definition A.2 (Ratios) A ratio is a quotient in which the numerator and denominator are measured using the same unit scales.

c.f. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ratio

Additional reference: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_algebra

A.2 Exponentials and Logarithms

Theorem A.14 (logarithm of a product is the sum of the logs of the factors) \[ \text{log}\left\{a\cdot b\right\} = \text{log}\left\{a\right\} + \text{log}\left\{b\right\} \]

Corollary A.1 (logarithm of a quotient)  

The logarithm of a quotient is equal to the difference of the logs of the factors:

\[\text{log}\left\{\frac{a}{b}\right\} = \text{log}\left\{a\right\} - \text{log}\left\{b\right\}\]

Theorem A.15 (logarithm of an exponential function) \[ \text{log}\left\{a^b\right\} = b \cdot\text{log}\left\{a\right\} \]

Theorem A.16 (exponential of a sum)  

The exponential of a sum is equal to the product of the exponentials of the addends:

\[\text{exp}\left\{a+b\right\} = \text{exp}\left\{a\right\} \cdot\text{exp}\left\{b\right\}\]

Corollary A.2 (exponential of a difference)  

The exponential of a difference is equal to the quotient of the exponentials of the addends:

\[\text{exp}\left\{a-b\right\} = \frac{\text{exp}\left\{a\right\}}{\text{exp}\left\{b\right\}}\]

Theorem A.17 (exponential of a product) \[a^{bc} = \left(a^b\right)^c = \left(a^c\right)^b\]

Corollary A.3 (natural exponential of a product) \[\text{exp}\left\{ab\right\} = (\text{exp}\left\{a\right\})^b = (\text{exp}\left\{b\right\})^a\]

Theorem A.18 (exp{} and log{} are mutual inverses) \[\text{exp}\left\{\text{log}\left\{a\right\}\right\} = \text{log}\left\{\text{exp}\left\{a\right\}\right\} = a\]

A.3 Derivatives

Theorem A.19 (Derivatives of polynomials) \[\frac{\partial}{\partial x}x^q = qx^{q-1}\]

Theorem A.20 (derivative of natural logarithm) \[\text{log}'\left\{x\right\} = \frac{1}{x} = x^{-1}\]

Theorem A.21 (derivative of exponential) \[\text{exp}'\left\{x\right\} = \text{exp}\left\{x\right\}\]

Theorem A.22 (Product rule) \[(ab)' = ab' + ba'\]

Theorem A.23 (Quotient rule) \[(a/b)' = 1/b - (a/b^2)b'\]

Theorem A.24 (Chain rule) \[\frac{\partial a}{\partial c} = \frac{\partial a}{\partial b} \frac{\partial b}{\partial c}\]

Corollary A.4 (Chain rule for logarithms) \[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\text{log}\left\{f(x)\right\} = \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)} \]

Proof. Apply Theorem A.24 and Theorem A.20.

A.4 Vector Calculus

(adapted from Fieller (2016), §7.2)

Let \(\tilde{x}\) and \(\tilde{\beta}\) be vectors of length \(p\), or in other words, matrices of length \(p \times 1\):

\[ \tilde{x}= \begin{bmatrix} x_{1} \\ x_{2} \\ \vdots \\ x_{p} \end{bmatrix} \\ \]

\[ \tilde{\beta}= \begin{bmatrix} \beta_{1} \\ \beta_{2} \\ \vdots \\ \beta_{p} \end{bmatrix} \]

Definition A.3 (Transpose) The transpose of a row vector is the column vector with the same sequence of entries:

\[ \tilde{x}' \equiv \tilde{x}^\top \equiv [x_1, x_2, ..., x_p] \]

Example A.1 (Dot product as matrix multiplication) \[ \begin{aligned} \tilde{x}\cdot \tilde{\beta} &= \tilde{x}^{\top} \tilde{\beta} \\ &= [x_1, x_2, ..., x_p] \begin{bmatrix} \beta_{1} \\ \beta_{2} \\ \vdots \\ \beta_{p} \end{bmatrix} \\ &= x_1\beta_1+x_2\beta_2 +...+x_p \beta_p \end{aligned} \]

Theorem A.25 (Transpose of a sum) \[(\tilde{x}+\tilde{y})^{\top} = \tilde{x}^{\top} + \tilde{y}^{\top}\]

Definition A.4 (Vector derivative of a vector-to-scalar function) If \(f(\tilde{\beta})\) is a function that takes a vector \(\tilde{\beta}\) as input and outputs a scalar, such as \(f(\tilde{\beta}) = x'\tilde{\beta}\), then:

\[ \frac{\partial}{\partial \tilde{\beta}} f(\tilde{\beta}) = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial}{\partial \beta_1}f(\tilde{\beta}) \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial \beta_2}f(\tilde{\beta}) \\ \vdots \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial \beta_p}f(\tilde{\beta}) \end{bmatrix} \]

Theorem A.26 (Derivative of a linear combination) \[ \frac{\partial}{\partial \tilde{\beta}} \tilde{x}^{\top} \tilde{\beta}= x \]

This looks a lot like non-vector calculus, except that you have to transpose the coefficient.

Proof. \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial}{\partial \beta} (x^{\top}\beta) &= \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial}{\partial \beta_1}(x_1\beta_1+x_2\beta_2 +...+x_p \beta_p ) \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial \beta_2}(x_1\beta_1+x_2\beta_2 +...+x_p \beta_p ) \\ \vdots \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial \beta_p}(x_1\beta_1+x_2\beta_2 +...+x_p \beta_p ) \end{bmatrix} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} x_{1} \\ x_{2} \\ \vdots \\ x_{p} \end{bmatrix} \\ &= \tilde{x} \end{aligned} \]

Definition A.5 (Quadratic form) A quadratic form is a mathematical expression with the structure

\[\tilde{x}^{\top} \mathbf{S} \tilde{x}\]

where \(\tilde{x}\) is a vector and \(\mathbf{S}\) is a matrix with compatible dimensions for vector-matrix multiplication.

Quadratic forms occur frequently in regression models. They are the matrix-vector generalizations of the scalar quadratic form \(cx^2 = xcx\).

Theorem A.27 (Derivative of a quadratic form) If \(S\) is a \(p\times p\) matrix that is constant with respect to \(\beta\), then:

\[ \frac{\partial}{\partial \beta} \beta'S\beta = 2S\beta \]

This is like taking the derivative of \(cx^2\) with respect to \(x\) in non-vector calculus.

Corollary A.5 (Derivative of a simple quadratic form) \[ \frac{\partial}{\partial \tilde{\beta}} \tilde{\beta}'\tilde{\beta}= 2\tilde{\beta} \]

This is like taking the derivative of \(x^2\).

## Vector Calculus {#sec-vector-calculus}

(adapted from @fieller2018basics, §7.2)

Let $\vx$ and $\vb$ be vectors of length $p$, 
or in other words, matrices of length $p \times 1$:

$$
\vx = \begin{bmatrix}
x_{1} \\
x_{2} \\
\vdots \\
x_{p}
\end{bmatrix} \\
$$

$$
\vb = \begin{bmatrix}
\beta_{1} \\
\beta_{2} \\
\vdots \\
\beta_{p}
\end{bmatrix}
$$

:::{#def-transpose}
#### Transpose

The transpose of a row vector is the column vector with the same 
sequence of entries:

$$
\vx' \equiv \vx^\top \equiv [x_1, x_2, ..., x_p]
$$

:::

:::{#exm-dot-product}
#### Dot product as matrix multiplication

$$
\begin{aligned}
\vx \cdot \vb
&= \vx \' \vb
\\ &= [x_1, x_2, ..., x_p] 
\begin{bmatrix}
\beta_{1} \\
\beta_{2} \\
\vdots \\
\beta_{p}
\end{bmatrix} 
\\ &= 
x_1\beta_1+x_2\beta_2 +...+x_p \beta_p 
\end{aligned}
$$
:::

:::{#thm-transpose-sum}
#### Transpose of a sum
$$(\vx+\vy)\' = \vx\' + \vy\'$$
:::

---

::: {#def-vector-derivative}
#### Vector derivative of a vector-to-scalar function
If $f(\vb)$ is a function that takes a vector $\vb$ as input and outputs a scalar, 
such as $f(\vb) = x'\vb$, 
then:

$$
\deriv{ \vb} f(\vb) = 
\begin{bmatrix}
\deriv{\beta_1}f(\vb) \\
\deriv{\beta_2}f(\vb) \\
\vdots \\
\deriv{\beta_p}f(\vb)
\end{bmatrix}
$$
:::

---

:::{#thm-deriv-lincom}

#### Derivative of a linear combination

$$
\deriv{\vb} \vx\' \vb = x
$$

:::: notes
This looks a lot like non-vector calculus, except that you have to transpose the coefficient.
::::
:::

---

::: proof

$$ 
\ba
\deriv{ \beta} (x\'\beta) 
&= 
\begin{bmatrix}
\deriv{\beta_1}(x_1\beta_1+x_2\beta_2 +...+x_p \beta_p ) \\
\deriv{\beta_2}(x_1\beta_1+x_2\beta_2 +...+x_p \beta_p ) \\
\vdots \\
\deriv{\beta_p}(x_1\beta_1+x_2\beta_2 +...+x_p \beta_p )
\end{bmatrix} 
\\ &=
\begin{bmatrix}
x_{1} \\
x_{2} \\
\vdots \\
x_{p}
\end{bmatrix}
\\ &= \vx
\ea
$$

:::

---

:::{#def-quadratic-form}
#### Quadratic form

A **quadratic form** is a mathematical expression with the structure

$$\vx\' \matr{S} \vx$$

where $\vx$ is a vector and $\matr{S}$ is a matrix with compatible dimensions for vector-matrix multiplication.

:::

::: notes
Quadratic forms occur frequently in regression models. They are the matrix-vector generalizations of the scalar quadratic form $cx^2 = xcx$.
:::

---

:::{#thm-quadratic-form}

#### Derivative of a quadratic form

If $S$ is a $p\times p$ matrix that is constant with respect to $\beta$, then:

$$
\deriv{\beta} \beta'S\beta = 2S\beta
$$

:::
 
::: notes
This is like taking the derivative of $cx^2$ with respect to $x$ in non-vector calculus.
:::

---

:::{#cor-deriv-normsq}

#### Derivative of a simple quadratic form

$$
\deriv{\vb} \vb'\vb = 2\vb
$$

:::

::: notes
This is like taking the derivative of $x^2$.
:::
